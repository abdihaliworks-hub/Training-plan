CYCLE STRUCTURE
WEEKS 1–2 · VOLUME
High climbing volume at moderate intensity. 3–4 sessions, lots of problems, build load tolerance. Strength A+B at full volume. You won't feel your best — that's expected and correct. You're making deposits.
WEEKS 3–4 · INTENSITY
Volume drops ~30%, load goes up. Max hangs, limit bouldering, campus work (cycle 2+). Strength maintained at fewer sets. You're converting accumulated base into usable power. Should feel sharp around week 4.
WEEK 5 · PEAK
Volume drops 40–50%, intensity stays high. 2–3 climbing sessions. One light strength session. CNS is fresh, adaptation has caught up. This is the fruit — go at your limits and see what the previous 4 weeks built.

SAUNA PROTOCOL
◆Post-session only — Wednesday post-climbing, Saturday post-Strength B
◆15–20 min at 80–100°C. One round sufficient; two with a cold shower between if feeling good
◆500ml+ water before entering. Electrolytes post if sweating heavily
◆Skip sauna the evening before Week 5 peak sessions — mild CNS cost not worth it
◆Good on rest days too — passive blood flow and recovery without training load
COLLAGEN PROTOCOL — EVERY CLIMBING SESSION

15g collagen hydrolysate + 50mg vitamin C, taken 30–60 min before the session. Keith Baar's research (UC Davis, 2017) shows this window significantly upregulates collagen synthesis in tendons during the subsequent loading period. Cheap, no downside, strong evidence base. Non-negotiable for the first two cycles while connective tissue is still ramping back up post-injury.

FULL ROUTINE · ~45 MIN
01
Couch Stretch
Rear knee on floor, front foot forward, upright torso. Drive hip forward. Critical for hip flexors shortened by sitting and by the hip flexion demands of climbing.
2 min each side
02
Frog Pose
Knees wide, feet in line with knees, rock forward and back gently. Hip external rotation. Directly improves high foot placements and hip-to-wall positioning on steep terrain.
3 min
03
Pancake Stretch
Seated straddle, fold forward with a flat back — hinge from the hip, don't round. Hamstrings and inner thigh flexibility. Improves drop-knee and flag positions significantly over time.
3 min
04
90/90 Hip Rotations
Both legs at 90° on floor, rotate between internal and external rotation, hold at end range each side. Comprehensive hip mobility — addresses both anterior and posterior capsule.
2 min each side
05
Thoracic Rotation over Foam Roller
Roller perpendicular to spine, segment-by-segment extension. Then seated rotation with arms crossed. T-spine mobility improves overhead reach and shoulder positioning — both critical for reachy problems.
4 min
06
PNF Hamstring Stretch
Lying on back, leg up. Contract hamstring against band or partner for 5s, relax and deepen the stretch. Repeat × 3 each side. PNF consistently outperforms static stretching for ROM gains.
3 min each side
07
Shoulder External Rotation Stretch
Doorframe or wall, arm at 90°, rotate body away. Counteracts the chronic internal rotation pattern from climbing. Should feel a deep stretch through the anterior shoulder and pec.
2 min each side
08
Overhead Band Distraction
Band anchored low, loop around wrist, reach overhead and side-bend away. Shoulder capsule distraction. Improves overhead lockoff range and reduces impingement risk over time.
2 min each side
09
Deep Squat Hold
Feet shoulder-width, heels down, hold bottom. Use a pole for assistance if needed initially. Ankle mobility, hip mobility, lower back decompression — one of the highest-ROI positions you can spend time in.
3 min cumulative
10
Wrist Flexor / Extensor Stretch
Both directions, arm straight. Add gentle loaded stretch on all fours (push through palm). Essential maintenance for finger flexor tendon health — especially relevant during your hand injury recovery window.
2 min each direction
OPTIONAL ADD-ONS

If time or a specific area needs it: Jefferson Curl (light weight, controlled spinal flexion) for posterior chain flexibility. Cossack squat for lateral hip mobility. Pike stretch for combined calf and hamstring. These are bonus — not required every week, but useful when something feels chronically tight.

CYCLE-TO-CYCLE PROGRESSION
Squat / Deadlift
+2.5–5kg per cycle. Conservative but compounds quickly. Don't chase weight at the cost of technique.
OHP / Barbell Row
+1.25–2.5kg per cycle. Upper body progresses slower — respect that and don't force it.
Hang weight
+2.5kg per cycle, or progress from BW to weighted once bodyweight hangs feel trivial. Track this number — it's one of the strongest climbing performance predictors.
Board / wall grades
Peak week ceiling should move up one step every 1–2 cycles. Let the base phases do the work — the grade rise should feel almost automatic in peak week, not forced.
Climbing volume
Add one session per week every 2 cycles if recovery permits. The natural jump is 3 → 4 sessions. Don't rush it.

WHEN A PEAK FEELS FLAT

Not every peak will be a breakthrough — some cycles you consolidate rather than jump. This is normal and doesn't mean the cycle failed. Trend across 3–4 mesocycles is what matters, not each individual peak. A flat peak typically means one of: insufficient volume weeks 1–2, poor sleep, nutrition lagging, or the body still adapting from the previous cycle. Identify which, adjust the next cycle, and move on.

SIGNS TO BACK OFF
◆Any sharp or localised pain in fingers, pulleys, or tendons during a session — stop immediately
◆Morning finger/forearm soreness that doesn't clear within 48 hours
◆3+ consecutive nights of poor sleep — performance and recovery both collapse; reduce load before it compounds
◆Grades dropping relative to the same phase last cycle — sign of accumulated fatigue, not fitness regression
